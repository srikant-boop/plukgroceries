# Pluk — Brand reference

Pluk is a small weekly market drop for Oakville. Real food and small-batch
non-food items, dropped at a community pickup spot, paid by card.

Use this file as the gut-check before changing copy, surfacing
information, or making stocking decisions.

The brand is **a decision procedure, not three coequal pillars.** They
form a hierarchy: items must pass the first gate, then optimize on the
second; the third is disclosure, never a tiebreaker.

---

## 1 · QUALITY *(gate)*

If an item doesn't meet the quality bar, it doesn't get stocked. Organic
is *not* the gate (insisting on it priced us out of being competitive).
The gate is real, fresh, well-made.

**Phase-1 definition (food):** top-grade fresh produce, inspected on the
wholesale floor. No close-to-expiry inventory, no seconds, no industrial
junk. Certified organic where available at a price we can offer — and
labeled clearly on the product page when so.

**For non-food** (crafts, candles, soaps when we stock them): well-made
with real materials — same spirit, different standards.

---

## 2 · COST *(the actual optimization)*

Among items that pass the quality gate, **best price wins.** Period.

This is what makes Pluk Pluk. We negotiate hard, take a thin markup
(Aldi-style, ~25%), and the customer gets the same real food for less
than at Whole Foods or Farm Boy.

### What this means in practice

- **Locality is not a tiebreaker.** A wholesaler in Mississauga at $4
  beats an Oakville farmer at $6 *every time* — assuming both pass the
  quality gate. We pick the wholesaler.
- **An imported item can win** over a local one if it's healthier and/or
  cheaper. We don't apologize for it.
- **The peer changes per item type:** broccoli's peer is Whole Foods +
  Farm Boy; a beeswax candle's peer is other small-batch candles; a
  craft tea's peer is other specialty teas. We always compare to *the
  appropriate peer*, not the cheapest possible equivalent.
- **We don't guarantee the lowest price every week** — when a big store
  has a sale we can't match, we mark "↑ $X more than Farm Boy" honestly
  on the page. Transparency is the promise. Always-winning is not.

---

## 3 · SOUL *(disclosure layer — never a differentiator)*

Every item has a who-and-where. We tell the story of whoever won the
price competition.

**Crucially:** soul does NOT drive stocking decisions. If a Burlington
beekeeper has the best honey at the best price, great — and they'll get
a beautiful supplier page. If a wholesaler with no obvious story has the
best price for our carrots, we still buy from the wholesaler. We just
write less prose about them.

### Depth of soul scales with what's available

| Type | Depth shown |
|---|---|
| Direct from a farmer | Full story, location, links, map |
| Direct from a maker | Full story, location, links, map |
| Through a wholesaler | Wholesaler name + box brand when visible (Chiquita, Sunkist, etc.) |
| Anonymous box at the OFT | Stall name and that's it |

We never write "from a supplier" — there's always *some* name.

---

## Discovery is a rhythm, not a pillar

Some items are weekly staples (broccoli, milk, eggs). Some rotate per
drop (a small-batch craft, a seasonal jar, a one-off find). **Both kinds
pass the same three rules above.** Discovery items just get fresher
prose each drop because the customer hasn't met that maker before.

Live in the "Discover" section on the homepage. Badge = `FIND`. Don't
call them "Neighbour" — that promises locality, which we may break the
moment a Chinese tea wholesaler beats a Burlington tea blender on price.

**Savings shows in exactly two places, no more.**

Showing "Save $X" on the card AND in the price area AND as a list AND
in a table is the loud-discount vibe we're explicitly avoiding. Aldi
doesn't do it; we shouldn't either.

- **Card (grocery item):** image + name + unit + price. A small
  strikethrough of the **cheapest competitor's price** appears under our
  price *only when we beat them.* If our price is at or above the
  cheapest peer, no strikethrough — we don't fake a comparison. Never
  use the most-expensive peer for the strikethrough (that's inflated
  flattery).
- **Card (Discover item):** image + name + unit + price. No
  strikethrough — Discover items aren't framed as deals. **No FIND chip
  on the card** either — the section header is already "Discover," and
  Discover items only appear in that section. Chip would be redundant.
  The FIND identity is preserved on the detail page subtitle line ("Find
  · from [supplier]") for anyone who lands there via direct link.
- **Detail page (grocery items with competitors):** price + add to cart
  + description + metadata strip + **comparison table** at the bottom.
  No "You save X%" headline. No per-row "Save $X vs" bullets above the
  table. The table itself carries the comparison data — that's enough.
- **Detail page (Discover items):** no comparison table. Short maker story
  on the page (`discoverBlurb` on the supplier — factual only). Link to
  the full supplier profile for the rest. Product copy is product facts
  only; no unverifiable sourcing or marketing claims.
- **Cart:** basket-level "you're saving" widget stays — one summary, at
  the moment of payment, not on every product surface. Includes specials
  (basket-level honesty without per-item framing).

## Voice

- **Concrete > clever.** "We pick up at the food terminal" beats
  "Reimagining the produce supply chain."
- **Founder voice.** First person, direct, warm — like the original FB
  post. No marketing-deck verbs.
- **No emoji** unless the item literally is one (the leaf icon stays as
  the brand mark, but only as a chrome element).
- **No marketing fluff.** Skip "elevating," "curated experience,"
  "passion for," "reimagining," "artisanal."
- **Show, don't promise.** "$5 cheaper than Whole Foods" beats
  "incredible value."

### Phrases to keep

- "Real food and the makers behind it"
- "Honest prices"
- "Small-batch find"
- "Weekly drop"
- "Aldi-thin markup" (in long copy only — FAQ, supplier pages)
- "Top-grade fresh" / "wholesale-floor fresh"
- "Certified organic" — only when the specific item is, never as a brand-wide claim
- "Discover"
- "From [supplier name]"

### Phrases to avoid

- **"No markups"** — we have a markup, just thin. Never claim zero.
- **"Splitting the actual cost"** — overclaims.
- **"From the neighbourhood" / "neighbouring farms"** as a structural
  promise — it boxes us out of taking a great-priced wholesaler from
  further away. Use only in *prose for a specific drop* when the supplier
  genuinely is a neighbour.
- **"Save big!" / "Amazing deal!"** — Aldi doesn't preach savings on
  every page. Numbers on the cards do that job. Pluk doesn't preach.
- **"Curated"** — overused; show what's in the drop instead.
- **"Premium"** — we're not premium-positioned.
- **"Supporting local"** as a Pluk reason-for-being — we'd ditch the
  local farmer for a better-priced healthy wholesaler. Saying we support
  local is dishonest by rule #2.

## Anti-drift checklist

Before adding any item to the catalog:
1. Does it pass the QUALITY gate? If no → reject.
2. Among items that pass, is this the best price vs the appropriate
   peer? If no → reject.
3. Can we name a who-and-where? If no → reject (we don't stock
   anonymous things).

Before changing any copy:
1. Does it express the relevant rule more honestly?
2. Does it use the voice rules?
3. Does it use any "phrases to avoid"?
4. Does it imply something we'd break the moment we change suppliers?
   (E.g., "from neighbours" — would we break that for cheaper imports?
   Yes → don't use as a structural promise.)

Before adding any UI element (badge, section, panel):
1. Is it expressing a real rule, or just decoration?
2. Could it lie in 4 weeks when the catalog shifts?
3. Is it duplicating something already visible?
